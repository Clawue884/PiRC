 PiRC Game Theory & Security Model

Rational Behavior, Adversarial Scenarios, and Economic Defense Layer

---

 1. Premise

All decentralized systems are governed by incentives, not intentions.

«If an action is profitable, it will be exploited.»

PiRC is designed under a strict assumption:

- Users are rational
- Actors maximize utility
- Adversaries actively seek system weaknesses

---

 2. Economic Game Model

Players

1. Pioneers (Contributors)
   
   - Provide real utility (apps, transactions, services)

2. Speculators
   
   - Seek profit via price movement (external markets)

3. Developers
   
   - Build applications and generate activity

4. Liquidity Providers
   
   - Supply capital to stabilize markets

5. Adversaries
   
   - Attempt to exploit reward, governance, or liquidity systems

---

Core Objective

Each player maximizes:

Utility = Rewards + Influence + Liquidity Access − Cost − Risk

---

 3. Attack Surface Overview

PiRC identifies four primary attack vectors:

1. Reward Exploitation
2. Liquidity Manipulation
3. Governance Capture
4. Oracle / Data Manipulation

---

 4. Attack Scenarios & Defense Mechanisms

---

4.1 Free Rider Problem

Attack:

Users attempt to gain rewards without meaningful contribution.

Examples:

- Fake activity loops
- Low-value spam transactions
- Passive holding with reward expectation

---

Defense:

 Weighted Contribution Factor (WCF)

- Rewards tied to:
  - Economic value
  - Time-weighted activity
  - Cross-app validation

 Diminishing Returns Curve

- Repetitive actions yield lower marginal rewards

 Behavioral Filtering

- Detect low-entropy patterns

---

Result:

«Free riding becomes economically unviable»

---

4.2 Sybil Attack (Multi-Account Farming)

Attack:

One actor creates multiple identities to farm rewards.

---

Defense:

 KYC Anchoring (Pi Native)

- Each identity linked to verified human

 Cross-Behavior Correlation

- Detect:
  - Shared activity patterns
  - Linked transaction graphs

 WCF Reputation Memory

- Long-term scoring penalizes shallow accounts

---

Result:

«Scaling fake identities becomes costly and inefficient»

---

4.3 Whale Manipulation

Attack:

Large holders attempt to:

- Control liquidity
- Manipulate price perception
- Dominate reward distribution

---

Defense:

 Quadratic Influence Model

- Influence ∝ √(stake), not linear

 WCF > Capital Priority

- Contribution outranks pure capital

 Liquidity Smoothing (Φ Engine)

- Dampens sudden market shocks

---

Result:

«Wealth does not equal control»

---

4.4 Liquidity Drain Attack

Attack:

Adversaries exploit pools by:

- Rapid buy/sell cycles
- Arbitrage draining internal liquidity

---

Defense:

 Adaptive Spread Control

- Spread widens under volatility

 Dynamic Fee Model

- Higher fees for high-frequency extraction

 Internal vs External Decoupling

- Macro Pi insulated from Micro-Pi speculation

---

Result:

«Extraction becomes self-penalizing»

---

4.5 Governance Capture

Attack:

Actors accumulate influence to:

- Manipulate parameters
- Redirect rewards
- Centralize control

---

Defense:

 Multi-Factor Governance

Voting power based on:

- WCF (contribution)
- Stake
- Time participation

 Time-Locked Decisions

- Prevent instant takeover

 Reputation Decay

- Inactive actors lose influence

---

Result:

«Governance remains distributed and merit-based»

---

4.6 Oracle Manipulation

Attack:

Feed false data into:

- Price feeds
- Activity metrics
- Reward calculations

---

Defense:

 Multi-Source Oracle Aggregation

- Data from multiple independent feeds

 Outlier Rejection

- Statistical filtering of anomalies

 Confidence Scoring

- Low-confidence data ignored

---

Result:

«Data corruption becomes statistically ineffective»

---

 5. Nash Equilibrium Design

PiRC is engineered so that:

«The optimal strategy for each participant is to behave honestly.»

---

Equilibrium Properties:

- Honest contribution → maximum reward
- Manipulation → reduced efficiency
- Exploitation → increasing cost

---

Outcome:

Honest Behavior > Exploit Strategy (Long-Term Utility)

---

PiRC is not static.

It continuously adapts via:

- Reinforcement learning models

- Real-time system metrics (Φ)

---

Adaptive Actions:

- Adjust reward curves
- Modify liquidity parameters
- Rebalance incentive structures

---

Result:

«The system evolves faster than attackers»

 7. Cross-Layer Defense Strategy

PiRC uses multi-layer protection:

---

Layer 1 — Behavioral (WCF)

- Detects user-level manipulation

Layer 2 — Economic (Φ Engine)

- Stabilizes macro conditions

Layer 3 — Protocol (Smart Contracts)

- Enforces rules automatically

Layer 4 — Data (Oracle Layer)

- Secures inputs

---

Combined Effect:

«No single point of failure»

Remaining risks:

- Unknown behavioral exploits

---

Mitigation:

- Continuous monitoring
- Community governance
